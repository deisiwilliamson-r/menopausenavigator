Module 1: understanding your body
What is happening in your body?
Menopause is a natural change. The problem is that no one ever explains what it really means. Let’s change that.
Your hormones are changing
Your ovaries make less estrogen and progesterone as you get older. These two hormones do a lot more than control your period. They also shape your sleep, your mood, your memory, and your bones. When they start dropping, that is called perimenopause. Once you have gone 12 full months in a row without a period, you have reached menopause.
For many women in our community this can start sooner, often in the early or mid 40s, and it can feel more intense. Years of carrying stress, racism, and poverty wear on the body over time.
Why it may feel harder for you
Years of stress from poverty, racism, trauma, or time in jail can speed up how the body ages on the inside. That means the hormone change may start sooner, and the symptoms can feel stronger than they would for someone who has not carried the same weight.
None of that is your fault. It is your body responding to what it has been through.
The three stages
Perimenopause: the years leading up to menopause, when your hormones start to shift. Your periods become uneven and symptoms can begin.
Menopause: the point at which you have gone 12 full months in a row without a period.
Postmenopause: all the years after menopause. Some symptoms may continue, and new health changes can show up over time.

What about how you are feeling inside?
The emotional symptoms of menopause are just as real as the physical ones. They are talked about much less, so many women end up blaming themselves for how they feel, without knowing that hormones are behind it.
If you have been feeling “off” inside, this may be why. When estrogen drops, it changes the chemicals in your brain. Those are the same chemicals that control mood, worry, and memory.
Brain fog
Forgetting things, trouble focusing, or feeling slow in your head. This happens because estrogen helps your brain work.
Low mood
Feeling sad, flat, or teary for no clear reason. This is tied to how estrogen shapes the chemicals in your brain that help you feel good.
Worry and anxiety
Feeling on edge or overwhelmed more than usual. This is tied to how hormones affect the way your body handles stress.
Easily upset
Feeling frustrated or short-tempered more easily than before. This is a real hormone symptom, not a change in who you are.
None of this means something is wrong with who you are, and none of it means you are losing your mind. These are body symptoms with real ways to treat them, and you deserve help with all of it.
Before we talk about options
We want to say something important first.
Many women in our community have been hurt or brushed off by healthcare: being told that your symptoms are an attitude problem, being rushed through visits, being denied medicine, or having your pain ignored. If any of that has happened to you, feeling careful or unsure makes full sense. It is a fair response to a system that has let you down.
This tool does not tell you what to do. It gives you information, helps you figure out what matters most to you, and lets you decide what feels right, on your own terms and in your own time.
You are the expert on your own body
No one knows what you have been through, what you are feeling, or what you need better than you do. The goal here is health autonomy. In plain words, that means having the knowledge and the confidence to make choices that feel right for you. It might mean seeing a provider, trying something on your own, or just understanding what is happening for the first time.
You deserve care, and you get to decide what that looks like.

Module 2: your options
What are your options?
There is no single right answer. Tap each option to open it. Each one explains what it is, what it helps with, what to think about, and how to get it in Chicago.
A reminder: These options are general information, not medical advice. Whether any of them is right for you depends on your own health history. A provider can help you decide what fits your body and your situation.
Hormone therapy (HT)
Works best for hot flashes and sleep
›
What it is
Hormone therapy replaces some of the estrogen (and sometimes progesterone) that your body is making less of. It comes as pills, patches, creams, rings, or gels. There are also low-dose versions if you are worried about side effects.
What it helps with
Hot flashes and night sweats: works better than any other choice
Sleep problems and feeling tired
Mood changes tied to hot flashes and poor sleep
Vaginal dryness and bladder problems
Helps protect bones over time when started early in menopause
Things to think about
HT is not right for everyone. Women with some cancers, blood clots in the past, or high blood pressure that is hard to control may be told not to use it. HT is used to relieve symptoms and protect bones. Major guidelines say it should not be started just to prevent heart disease. A provider can help you sort out what fits you.
How to get this in Chicago
HT is offered at clinics that take Medicaid or charge by what you can pay. Find a City of Chicago Health Center › to start. These centers offer free language help on the spot. You can also see a menopause provider by phone through Midi Health (takes Medicaid, no in-person visit needed).
A question you could ask your health care provider
“Given my health history, could hormone therapy work for me? What are the risks for someone like me?”
Non-hormone medicines
Prescription options without hormones
›
What it is
Some medicines made for other health needs also help with menopause symptoms. These include low-dose mood medicines, a blood pressure medicine called clonidine, and a newer medicine (Veozah) made just for hot flashes.
What it helps with
Hot flashes and night sweats: can cut how often they happen
Mood changes, worry, and low feelings
Trouble sleeping
Things to think about
These need a prescription. Some take a few weeks to reach their full effect. If you already take medicine for mood or blood pressure, a provider needs to check that these are safe to take together.
How to get this in Chicago
Any health care provider can prescribe these. CommunityHealth Chicago offers free regular care for women without insurance. Midi Health can also prescribe non-hormone options by phone.
A question you could ask your health care provider
“Are there non-hormone medicine options that could lower my hot flashes without affecting the medicines I already take?”
Lifestyle changes
Things you can start on your own today
›
What it is
Small changes to your daily life that studies show can make symptoms feel better. No prescription, no visit, no cost. These work best along with other options when symptoms are strong.
What helps and why
Keep your bedroom cool. Use a fan, light blankets, or a cool shower before bed. A cooler body means fewer hot flashes.
Move your body. Regular movement supports sleep, mood, bones, and heart during menopause. Studies have not shown that walking alone reliably lowers hot flashes, but strength training may help.
Slow breathing during a hot flash. Some women find it helps them feel calmer in the moment. Large studies have not shown that it shortens the flash itself, but it can make the moment feel less scary.
Cut back on alcohol and caffeine. Both can set off hot flashes.
Go to bed and wake up at the same time each day. This helps your body’s sleep clock and makes sleep better over time.
Things to think about
These take time, and you have to do them often. They help more with mild symptoms than very strong ones. They work best as part of a bigger plan.
A question you could ask your health care provider
“Which lifestyle changes would help most for my specific symptoms?”
Other approaches
Herbs, acupuncture, and mind-body practices
›
Options with research behind them
Talk therapy (CBT). Strong evidence shows it helps with sleep, mood, and how much hot flashes bother you during menopause.
Mindfulness and stress relief. These help make symptoms feel less upsetting, even if the symptoms keep happening just as often.
Acupuncture. Some studies suggest a small benefit, but the findings are mixed.
Things to think about
Herbs and pills you buy at the store are not checked as closely as prescription medicines. They can also clash with other medicines you take. Always tell any provider you see about any herbs or pills you use. The resources at the end of this tool can help you find low-cost mental health and other providers.
How to get this in Chicago
Check the resources at the end of this tool for free and low-cost mental health and other care near you. The CHARM Center at UIC may have help too.
A question you could ask your health care provider
“Are these other options safe with the medicines I already take?”

ⓘ About this tool
About this tool
The Menopause Navigator was built to give clear, evidence-based information about menopause and the choices you have for care. Every section was written from current national menopause practice guidelines and federal women’s health resources. The clinical content was reviewed by the DNP project committee (board-certified clinicians) as part of standard DNP project oversight before the tool was made available to participants.
